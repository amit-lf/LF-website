The Questions Your Contracts Force

The same read, applied to your own book of contracts
Tap or hover any underlined term for a plain-English definition.

The first two Readers looked outward — nine semiconductor companies, read from their own filed contracts, and then a check on how those reads aged. This one turns the lens around. The method doesn't depend on those nine; it works on any book of contracts — yours, or a company you are about to fund or buy. And its output isn't a verdict or a score. It's a set of questions — the ones a company's own agreements force it, and its funders, to answer.

Why that matters now: a company with no plans to raise, sell, or grow can leave these questions closed — for them, contract hygiene really is a vitamin. But the moment you plan to raise a round, go public, get acquired, or defend a margin, someone else will ask these questions — an investor's counsel, an acquirer's diligence team, a lender. The terms that sat dormant become the clauses used against you at the table. Far better to hold the answers before they are demanded.

The method, in brief

Read the actual agreements — not the pitch — and resolve five things: who controls the company; whether it owns or rents its core technology; whether its supply is single-threaded; whether its revenue is committed or at-will; and what unwinds on a change of controlA change in who ultimately owns a company (e.g. an acquisition), which can trigger contract rights.. Each answer traces to a specific clause. Where the contracts alone can't settle it, that gap becomes a question to put to management. The result is a diligence agenda, grounded in the company's own paper.

A worked example: Allegro MicroSystems

Allegro went public as a US sensor-chip company. Read its filed agreements and the questions write themselves — and, tellingly, several of them came due in real life within a few years.

Control — and what happens when the parent leaves. Allegro's key rights are tied to its Japanese parent, Sanken: the foundryA factory that manufactures chips designed by others (e.g. TSMC, GlobalFoundries). collaboration can't be terminated or materially altered "without consultation with and concurrence of Sanken," and a royalty-sharing agreement "shall terminate in the event that Sanken ceases to own, directly or indirectly, more than fifty percent." The question a funder must ask: what is the plan the day Sanken falls below 50%? This was not hypothetical — in July 2024 Sanken repurchased down from 50.8% to 32.5%, and within months Allegro was the target of a roughly $6.9 billion acquisition bid from onsemi. The question the contract raised became the company's defining event. (Correlation, not causation — but the contract told you where to look.)

Own or rent the technology. The IP is split: magnetic-sensor IP is "solely owned by Allegro," while "all other IP will be jointly owned by Sanken and Allegro." Question: what can Allegro actually license, spin out, or sell on its own — and what needs the (now former-majority) parent's sign-off?

Single-threaded supply. Wafers come from Polar under a foundry agreement with a "six month binding forecast" and a liability capA ceiling on how much a party can be made to pay if things go wrong. that protects the supplier. Question: is Polar an arm's-length foundry, or a captive whose pricing and capacity the parent influences — and where is the second sourceA qualified alternative supplier for the same part.?

Committed or at-will revenue. That binding forecast is a take-or-payA promise to pay for a set volume whether or not you actually order it. commitment — Allegro must pay for the forecasted wafers whether or not it needs them. Question: how much inventory risk does it carry in a downturn, and does that exposure sit on Allegro's balance sheet?

Change of control. With parent-linked termination triggers embedded in key agreements, question: which contracts reprice, terminate, or require consent in an acquisition — i.e., what would an acquirer's diligence team find that changes the price? This is precisely the terrain a bidder walks.

The point — and the offer

None of these is a gotcha. Each is a question a serious investor or acquirer will ask, and each is answerable — from the contracts for some, from management for the rest. That is the deliverable: not a verdict on Allegro, but the agenda its own paper sets. We can produce the same agenda for any book of contracts — a company's own, or a target's — grounded clause by clause, from real filings.

For a company heading into a raise, an IPO, or a sale, the choice is simple: answer these questions on your own schedule, or on the other side's.
